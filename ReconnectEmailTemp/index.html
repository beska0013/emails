Gaucher disease (GD) is a rare, autosomal recessive genetic condition that usually manifests in childhood with thrombocytopenia, hepatosplenomegaly, and growth delay. A better understanding of typical presentations at different ages can lead to faster diagnosis and treatment.

There are three major types of GD: type 1 (non-neuronopathic), type 2 (acute neuronopathic form), and type 3 (chronic neuronopathic):

  • GD1 can be diagnosed at any age, whereas GD2 and GD3 are commonly diagnosed in infancy and early childhood, respectively.
  • In children, GD3 may be differentiated from GD1 by the presence of neurologic symptoms such as gaze abnormalities.

GD3 clinical manifestations in children can be misclassified as GD1, or misdiagnosed as a hematologic disease such as leukemia. These conditions should be carefully ruled out during differential diagnosis.

Disease-specific tests confirm the diagnosis of GD:

  • Acid Î²-glucosidase 1 enzyme activity assay in peripheral blood leukocytes or mononuclear cells, or cultured skin fibroblasts, and/or
  • GBA genetic testing to identify biallelic pathogenic variants; the finding of certain variants can be useful to support a diagnosis of non-neuronopathic or neuronopathic types.

Parents should be informed of the treatment options available (disease and non-disease-specific) and receive appropriate genetic testing and counseling.

After treatment initiation, children with GD require thorough, regular multimodality monitoring, including assessments of growth velocity, bone, spleen and liver imaging, and pain and QoL assessments.

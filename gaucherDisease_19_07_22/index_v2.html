Gaucher disease (GD) is a rare genetic condition encompassing a continuum of phenotypes, ranging from death in early infancy to adult asymptomatic patients.

There are three types of GD: type 1 (non-neuronopathic), type 2 (acute neuronopathic), and type 3 (chronic neuronopathic).

The main clinical manifestations of GD type 1 include hepato/splenomegaly, thrombocytopenia, anemia, and bone disease.

GD type 1 accounts for 90−95% of GD cases in Europe, North America, Latin America, and Israel. Its prevalence is higher in individuals with Ashkenazi Jewish ethnicity (birth incidence ~1:850), compared to the general population, (standardized incidence 0.39–5.80:100,000).

Some GD manifestations can be similar to those of other metabolic and non-metabolic disorders, particularly hematologic diseases. Careful differential diagnosis is crucial to avoid GD misdiagnosis – for instance, as leukemia or multiple myeloma – due to a shared set of common manifestations.

Disease-specific tests confirm the diagnosis of GD:

  • Acid β-glucosidase 1 enzyme activity assay in peripheral blood leukocytes or mononuclear cells, or cultured skin fibroblasts, and/or
  • GBA genetic testing to identify biallelic pathogenic variants.

Additional clinical evaluations are recommended, including serum calcium and vitamin D levels, disease biomarkers, and abdominal and bone imaging.

Patients should be informed of available treatment options (disease and non-disease-specific) to improve disease manifestations and slow down progression, and first-degree relatives should receive appropriate genetic counseling and testing.

Mucopolysaccharidosis (MPS) I is an autosomal recessive disease, caused by pathogenic variants in the IDUA gene, resulting in glycosaminoglycan (GAG) deposition. It is mainly diagnosed from early infancy to early adulthood.

It comprises severe (progressive cognitive impairment) and attenuated forms (mild or no cognitive impairment).

Common signs of attenuated MPS I include short stature, corneal clouding, joint contractures (without inflammation), cardiac valve disease, hernias, coarse facies, dysostosis multiplex, and hepatosplenomegaly.

  • Symptoms of MPS I overlap with those of other lysosomal storage disorders, which should be considered as part of the differential diagnosis.
  • MPS I is under-recognized, and its diagnosis remains delayed, particularly in patients with attenuated disease, often misdiagnosed as juvenile idiopathic/rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory, autoimmune, neuromuscular, or metabolic diseases.

Diagnosis is confirmed by the detection of deficient IDUA enzyme activity in combination with laboratory findings of elevated GAG levels, and/or identification of biallelic pathogenic variants in the IDUA gene.

Patients and parents/guardians should be informed of the potential disease management options available (disease and non-disease-specific), and receive genetic testing and counseling.

After diagnosis, patients require thorough, regular multidisciplinary monitoring

Mucopolysaccharidosis (MPS) I is an autosomal recessive disease, caused by pathogenic variants in IDUA, resulting in glycosaminoglycan (GAG) deposition.

It comprises severe (progressive cognitive impairment) and attenuated forms (mild or no cognitive impairment).

Common signs of attenuated MPS I include short stature, corneal clouding, joint contractures (without inflammation), cardiac valve disease, hernias, coarse facies, dysostosis multiplex, and hepatosplenomegaly.

  • Heart valve disease is the most prominent and consistent cardiac manifestation of patients with attenuated MPS (~70% of cases).
  • Valve involvement is present in almost all patients with MPS I and II.
  • Symptoms of MPS I overlap with those of other lysosomal storage disorders, which should be considered as part of the differential diagnosis.
  • MPS I is under-recognized, and its diagnosis remains delayed, particularly in attenuated disease, often misdiagnosed as inflammatory, autoimmune, neuromuscular, or other metabolic diseases.

Diagnosis is confirmed by the detection of deficient IDUA enzyme activity in combination with laboratory findings of elevated GAG levels, and/or identification of biallelic pathogenic variants in the IDUA gene.

  • Negative total urinary GAGs in individuals with attenuated disease does not exclude MPS I.

Patients and parents/guardians should be informed of the potential disease management options (disease and non-disease-specific), and receive genetic testing and counseling.
